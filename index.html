A Glimpse into Malaysia's Tourism

Introduction

Tourism plays a vital role in Malaysia’s economy, connecting cultures and driving growth across states and industries. However, like many countries, Malaysia’s tourism sector faced severe disruption during the COVID-19 pandemic. This visualisation explores how Malaysia’s tourism has grown and recovered between 2015 and 2024. Each chart reveals a different aspect of Malaysia’s journey towards regaining its position as one of Asia’s leading travel destinations.

The Impact of the Pandemic

The COVID-19 pandemic had a profound effect on Malaysia’s tourism sector. In 2021, international arrivals collapsed from 2019 — while domestic tourism became the nation’s main economic lifeline. As international borders remained closed, local travel slowly reignited. The following charts illustrate how both markets evolved between 2019 and 2024, highlighting Malaysia’s gradual road to recovery.

Tourism Hotspots Across Malaysia

In 2024, Malaysia’s tourism industry demonstrated a remarkable rebound with both local and international visitors contributing to the rebound across the nation. The map on the left highlights domestic tourism patterns — showing that Selangor remains the most visited state, followed closely by Johor and Sabah. These areas benefited from their strong urban centers and popular attractions. In contrast, Labuan and Perlis recorded the lowest domestic travel figures, reflecting smaller populations and limited tourism infrastructure.

On the right, international travel is shown to be concentrated in Selangor, and Johor. Meanwhile, states such as Perlis and Kelantan saw the fewest international arrivals. Together, these maps illustrate how Malaysian tourism works with domestic travelers tending to visit other states, while metropolitan states like Johor and Selangor attract international visitors.

Where Tourists Spend Their Money (2019–2024)

Between 2019 and 2021, Malaysia’s tourism economy nearly came to a halt, with expenditure plummeting across all major categories. By 2024, both domestic and international visitors had reignited the sector — collectively spending over RM 200 billion. These charts highlight how Malaysians and foreign tourists allocated their spending over time, revealing what sectors benefited most from the nation’s tourism revival.

Conclusion

Malaysia’s tourism journey between 2019 and 2024 paints a story of resilience and adaptability. When the pandemic brought travel to a standstill, both domestic and international arrivals collapsed to record lows. Yet within just a few years, the nation not only reopened its borders but reignited its tourism heartbeat.

The data reveals that domestic tourism played a crucial role in stabilising the industry during the lockdown period. Local travellers sustained hotels, restaurants, and cultural attractions, helping small businesses survive until international travel resumed. By 2024, international arrivals once again surpassed millions, with tourism expenditure rebounding beyond pre-pandemic levels which marks a full recovery for Malaysia’s visitor economy.

In essence, Malaysia’s post-pandemic tourism recovery showcases not just the return of travel, but the evolution of traveller behaviour. Spending has diversified toward experiences, local products, and cultural engagement, highlighting a shift from mass tourism toward a more sustainable consumer model.
